General First AID

Cuts and Scrapes

Minor cuts and scrapes can usually be treated at home.

  1. Wash your hands thoroughly.
  2. Clean the wound with mild soap and water.
  3. Apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment if available.
  4. Cover the wound with a clean bandage if necessary.
  5. Seek medical attention if the cut is deep, bleeding doesn't stop, or there are signs of infection.

Minor Burns

For minor burns (first-degree burns affecting only the outer layer of skin):

  1. Immediately cool the burn with cool (not ice-cold) running water for 10-15 minutes.
  2. Do not apply ice, butter, or ointments to the burn.
  3. Cover the burn loosely with a sterile, nonstick bandage.
  4. Seek medical attention for severe burns (second or third degree), burns on the face, hands, feet, or genitals, or if the burn covers a large area.

Sprains and Strains

Sprains (ligament injuries) and strains (muscle or tendon injuries) can often be managed with R.I.C.E.

  1. Rest: Avoid using the injured limb.
  2. Ice: Apply ice packs for 15-20 minutes at a time, several times a day.
  3. Compression: Wrap the injured area with a bandage to provide support.
  4. Elevation: Keep the injured limb raised above your heart if possible.
  5. Seek medical attention if the pain is severe, you can't put weight on the limb, or there is significant swelling or bruising.

Nosebleeds

Most nosebleeds are not serious and can be stopped at home.

  1. Sit upright and lean slightly forward.
  2. Pinch the soft part of your nose (below the bony bridge) firmly for 10-15 minutes.
  3. Breathe through your mouth.
  4. Do not tilt your head back.
  5. If the bleeding doesn't stop after 15 minutes, seek medical attention.

Choking (Adult and Older Child)

If someone is choking and cannot cough, speak, or breathe:

  1. Ask "Are you choking? Can you speak?"
  2. If they cannot speak, tell someone to call for emergency help immediately.
  3. Perform the Heimlich maneuver (abdominal thrusts) until the object is dislodged or the person becomes unconscious. (Note: Learn the proper technique from a certified instructor).
  4. If the person becomes unconscious, start CPR if you are trained.

Disclaimer: This is a basic overview. Proper training is essential for performing the Heimlich maneuver and CPR effectively.

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) - Basic Overview

CPR is an emergency procedure for someone whose heart has stopped beating. This is a very basic overview and does not replace proper CPR training.

  1. Check for responsiveness and breathing. If the person is not responsive and not breathing or only gasping, call for emergency help immediately.
  2. Place the person on their back on a firm surface.
  3. Push hard and fast in the center of the chest at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute. Push down about 2 inches for adults.
  4. If you are trained in CPR, give rescue breaths after every 30 chest compressions.
  5. Continue CPR until emergency help arrives.

Disclaimer: Learning CPR from a certified instructor is highly recommended.

Head Injuries (Minor)

For minor bumps and bruises to the head:

  1. Apply a cold compress to the area to reduce swelling.
  2. Watch for signs of more serious injury, such as loss of consciousness, severe headache, vomiting, confusion, or changes in vision or speech.
  3. Seek immediate medical attention if any of these danger signs are present.
